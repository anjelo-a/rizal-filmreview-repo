# Reflection & Critical Analysis

Before we watched this movie, we thought of Rizal as someone who's a hero but did not really know him as a person and only knew things about him from our textbooks. However, we did not really understand what he was like as a person. Seeing the movie changed that. Albert Martinez did a good job playing Jose Rizal, showing him as someone who is smart and has good values, but also someone who is lonely, frustrated, and in love. He is not perfect, he makes mistakes, and sometimes he is not sure about things, and that makes him feel like a real person. We really like the way the movie was filmed. The camera work makes the movie feel personal and they filmed the movie in Dapitan, where he actually lived, making it feel more real. There is a scene where he stands on the beach, gazing out at the ocean. The camera captures this moment in his presence, showing that this minimalistic approach effectively conveys his emotions and inner thoughts. What we think the movie does well is show that he was a hero in ways of what he did at the end of his life. He did things every day that showed he was a person, teaching kids and helping people who were sick. He tried to make the world a better place even when it was hard, which is really impressive because it shows that he really believed in what he was doing and he kept doing it without needing applause from anyone. 

The movie is not perfect though. While it does delve deeply into Rizal's relationship with Josephine, which is understandable since she held great significance in his life, the heavy focus on their love story comes at the cost of other important aspects of his exile, such as his writings and his broader intellectual work. This approach is effective since most people can relate to a love story. The movie uses the love story to convey his life and experiences, making it a compelling narrative. We also think the film does a great job of showing why he refused to join the Katipunan. The film does not make him look bad but shows his reasoning. He genuinely believed the country was not ready. Whether he was right or wrong is a question. The film respects his position and this is more nuanced than many historical films manage. The film presents him as an individual grappling with a challenging decision. One more thing that struck, was how the film shows his work in Dapitan as meaningful. He was exiled and was not merely passing the time but genuinely making a difference in people's lives. The children he educated, the patients he healed, and the water system he constructed which had a profound impact. The film suggests that perhaps Rizal's true legacy lies not in his writings or his martyrdom but how he lived his life and the profound influence he had on those around him. This perspective on heroism diverges from the understanding often found in textbooks.
