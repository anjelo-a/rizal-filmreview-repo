# Connection to Class Discussion

Watching this film actually made a lot of the stuff we talked about in class make more sense. Recalling our discussion about the Spanish government’s use of exile as a means to suppress dissent. The film illustrates this concept effectively. Rizal’s exile is not merely confined to a distant location; he is sent to the most remote place imaginable which is Dapitan. It's like the Spanish government is saying, "We're going to send you so far away that you can't influence anyone." But what's brilliant about him is that he doesn't just sit around feeling sorry for himself but uses his exile as an opportunity to do good work. He teaches, heals, and builds.

Things from Class That the Film Really Shows:

Exile as punishment: The Spanish didn't just arrest Rizal; they sent him far away to isolate him. The film shows how isolating that must have been, especially in the beginning when he's alone in this remote town.

Education as the answer: Rizal believed that if people were educated, they could solve their own problems. That's why he started a school even in exile and the film shows him teaching kids, and you can see how much he cares about it. It's not just something he does to pass the time; it's his philosophy in action.

Church power: The fact that the Church refused to marry Rizal and Josephine shows how much control the Church had. They could literally prevent people from getting married if they didn't approve. That shows not just a personal rejection but an abuse of power. The film shows how humiliating that must have been for him.

The split between reform and revolution: The visit from Pio Valenzuela is the turning point where you see the conflict between Rizal's peaceful approach and the Katipunan's revolutionary approach.
And what's interesting is that the film doesn't make him look stupid for refusing. It shows that he had reasons, because he thought the people weren't ready. Whether he was right or wrong is debatable, but the film respects his reasoning.

We think what's admirable is that the film doesn't make him look like he was wrong for refusing to join the revolution but shows that he had reasons for his position. He thought the people weren't ready and that they needed education first. Whether or not he was right about that is debatable, the Katipunan did eventually start the revolution without him, and it happened anyway. But the film respects his reasoning. It doesn't just make him look stubborn or cowardly. It shows him as someone trying to navigate an impossible situation where both options, showing that reform and revolution have serious consequences.
