# Connection to Class Discussion

The film connects closely with our class discussions about how Rizal's life should be understood beyond memorized facts and dates. In class, we often examine Rizal as a thinker whose nationalism was expressed not only through writing, but also through concrete service to the community. *Rizal in Dapitan* supports that idea by showing how teaching, medicine, and civic work became forms of resistance under colonial rule.

Another important connection is our discussion of the role of education in social change. Rizal's work with young students in Dapitan reflects the same belief we talk about in class: that learning can build critical thinking, dignity, and a sense of national identity. The film therefore turns classroom concepts into lived experiences, making Rizal's principles easier to visualize and understand.

The movie also supports our conversations about the tension between reform and revolution. Rather than presenting Rizal as a loud or purely militant hero, it shows him as disciplined, reflective, and strategic. This helps reinforce the class idea that nationalism can take many forms, including intellectual leadership, moral courage, and service to others.
